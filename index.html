What is this simulator?
Important Scientific Scope: This simulator represents a machine-learning benchmark constructed on a hypothetical generic dark-matter sensor-array layout. All event traces, correlations, and performance limits are derived from a synthetic Monte Carlo signal generator rather than physical measurements. While the classifier analyses (e.g. background discrimination limits, thermal scaling, and feature ablations) demonstrate high internal consistency within the framework of this simulation, the results do not represent actual detector physics data, nor do they serve as physical exclusion limits or experimental evidence for B-L vector dark matter in nature.
This tool models a proposed dark matter detector: an array of mechanical sensors made from Silicon and Iron, cooled to near absolute zero. It is based on hypothetical sensor behavior described in Carney et al. (2021). You can simulate how different synthesized processes leave distinct numeric "fingerprints" across the sensor array — and explore how a machine learning classifier evaluates them. Start with the ⚡ Event Topology Lab tab.
01
The Core Idea
Dark matter may interact via a B−L force (baryon minus lepton). If so, every atom in a sensor feels a tiny oscillating force at frequency f = 10¹² Hz × (m / 1 meV). This force is unimaginably small. The solution: use many sensors, cool them down, and look for the pattern across the array, not just amplitude.
02
Why Two Materials?
Silicon: Z/A = 14/28 = 0.500. Iron: Z/A = 26/56 = 0.464. DM couples differently to Si vs Fe. Seismic vibrations couple identically to all materials. So the differential Si−Fe signal cancels seismic noise but preserves DM. This is an equivalence-principle violation test.
03
The Event Topology Lab
Instead of just "how sensitive is the detector?", this lab asks: "What pattern does each event leave in the array?" Thermal is random. Seismic is uniform. DM has Si/Fe material-dependence. A traversing particle hits sensors in sequence. These topologies are distinguishable — the Lab shows you how and classifies each event.
04
The Galactic Wind
Earth moves through the DM halo at ~220 km/s toward Cygnus. This creates a preferred direction for DM arrival. As Earth rotates, sensors pointing toward Cygnus see higher DM flux — a 24-hour sidereal modulation. No background noise modulates with sidereal time. This is a powerful DM signature.

A different question from sensitivity analysis: instead of asking "how small a signal can we detect?", this lab asks "what pattern does each physical process leave in the sensor array — and can we reconstruct the source?" Adjust the mixture of physical events and evaluate how robust the classifiers are against complex backgrounds.
⚠️ PHYSICAL REALISM & SIMULATION ASSUMPTIONS DETAILED: This dashboard presents idealized benchmark conditions constructed on a synthetic Monte Carlo signal model. While the classifier analyses exhibit excellent consistency, the simulated DM signal model is heavily built around a single engineered parameter, Material Asymmetry (v_var), to produce separation. Real-world experimental environments are subject to complex confounding factors like calibration drift, detector electronics noise, and correlated environmental backgrounds. These would introduce substantial noise, resulting in much messier boundaries than the exceptionally clean performance metrics (e.g., FPR ≈ 0–0.3%) achieved within the present simulation framework.
